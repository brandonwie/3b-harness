# LinkedIn Post Guide — Paper Insights

Format for sharing research paper insights on LinkedIn. The primary audience
includes recruiters, hiring managers, and engineering leaders — people
evaluating your technical depth and curiosity, not peers who want a paper
summary.

## Purpose (CRITICAL)

LinkedIn posts are **professional signals**, not content marketing. They show:

- You actively read research and stay current
- You can distill complex ideas into clear, practical language
- You think critically about how research applies to real engineering

**LinkedIn ≠ Blog.** The blog is your public documentation space (detailed,
educational, personal). LinkedIn is a professional window (compact, objective,
informative). A recruiter scanning your profile should think "this person reads
papers and thinks clearly" — not "this person writes long posts."

## Tone

| Do                                 | Do NOT                     |
| ---------------------------------- | -------------------------- |
| Objective, matter-of-fact          | Hype or sensationalize     |
| Compact (under 200 words)          | Ramble or over-explain     |
| Informative — what the paper found | Brag about reading papers  |
| Professional curiosity             | Performative enthusiasm    |
| Plain language, no jargon          | Academic language          |
| Let the research speak             | Insert opinions everywhere |

**Voice model:** Think conference hallway — brief, clear, interesting. Not a
keynote, not a tweet thread.

## Structure (100-200 words)

### Line 1: What the paper found (1 sentence)

Lead with the insight, not the paper title. Factual, specific.

- Good: "Reasoning models internally simulate multi-agent debates — they don't
  just 'think longer.'"
- Bad: "Just read an amazing paper that blew my mind! 🤯"
- Bad: "Everyone is doing X wrong. This paper proves it."

### Core (3-5 lines): Why it matters practically

Explain the finding in engineering terms. One concrete implication. No
editorializing — just connect the dot between research and practice.

### Close (1-2 lines): Link + context tag

Paper link. One sentence of professional context if relevant ("Relevant to work
I'm doing on X" or "Interesting for anyone building Y"). No call to action, no
"what do you think?" engagement bait.

### AI Disclosure (REQUIRED)

Every post MUST include an AI disclosure line before the link. Non-negotiable —
transparency about AI assistance in content creation.

Format: `- analyzed and drafted with Claude`

Place on its own line, right before the paper link.

### Hashtags (1 line, 2-3 tags max)

Fewer is better. Only domain-relevant tags:

- 1 domain: `#AI`, `#MachineLearning`, or `#SoftwareEngineering`
- 1 specific: `#RAG`, `#LLM`, `#FineTuning`, `#AgenticAI`
- 1 optional: `#AIEngineering`

No `#BuildInPublic`, `#LearningInPublic`, `#motivation` — these signal content
creator, not engineer.

## Example Post

```text
Reasoning models like DeepSeek-R1 don't improve by "thinking longer" — they
internally simulate multi-agent debates among distinct cognitive perspectives.
Evans et al. call this a "society of thought."

The practical implication: scaling AI may be less about bigger models and more
about composing multiple agents into structured ensembles. The paper draws
parallels to how human intelligence scaled through social organization, not
individual brain size.

Relevant to anyone designing multi-agent architectures or agent orchestration
systems.

- analyzed and drafted with Claude

Paper: https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.20639

#AI #AgenticAI
```

## Anti-patterns

- Do NOT open with "I just read..." or "Excited to share..."
- Do NOT use emojis (they undermine professional tone)
- Do NOT ask the audience a question (engagement bait)
- Do NOT tag authors unless you know them personally
- Do NOT exceed 200 words (shorter = more likely to be read fully)
- Do NOT list multiple takeaways — pick the ONE most interesting finding
- Do NOT mention your blog post in the LinkedIn post (different audiences,
  different purposes)
- Do NOT use "we" or "our" when summarizing the paper — you didn't write it

## Language

English only. Professional, third-person reporting tone. Avoid first person
except in the optional context line at the close.
